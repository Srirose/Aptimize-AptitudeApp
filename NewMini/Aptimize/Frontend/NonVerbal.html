Mirror Image Concepts

Mirror Image:

Involves finding the mirror image of a given figure. Common rules include:

Vertical Mirror Image: The left and right sides of the object are swapped. For example:

  • If the letter "P" is reflected in a vertical mirror, it becomes "Ôš".
  • In a vertical mirror, the object's reflection is flipped horizontally.

Horizontal Mirror Image: The top and bottom sides of the object are swapped. For example:

  • If the letter "E" is reflected in a horizontal mirror, it still remains "E", but flipped upside down.

Shape Symmetry: Some objects have symmetrical reflections along a mirror. For example:

  • A symmetrical shape like a square or an equilateral triangle remains unchanged when reflected in a mirror.
  • Asymmetrical shapes like letters or objects may look completely different when reflected in a mirror.

Water Image Concepts

Water Image:

Water Image problems involve understanding how an object looks when reflected in a body of water. Common rules include:

Vertical Reflection: The object is flipped vertically, but its left and right sides remain unchanged.

  • If the letter "P" is placed in water, it becomes "Ôš" (the bottom part is reflected).
  • The water image involves flipping the object along the horizontal axis.

Horizontal Reflection: The object is flipped along the horizontal axis, resulting in a mirror image from top to bottom.

  • If the letter "A" is placed in water, it remains "A" (since it has vertical symmetry).
  • Objects with horizontal symmetry remain unchanged in the water image.

Shape Symmetry: Some shapes or letters remain the same when reflected in water due to symmetry. For example:

  • Letters like "A", "H", "I", "M", "O", "T", "U", "V", "W", "X", and "Y" have vertical symmetry, meaning they remain unchanged in a water image.
  • Asymmetrical objects or letters, like "B", "C", "D", and "E", will appear different in the water image.

Pattern Completion Concepts

Pattern Completion:

Pattern Completion problems involve identifying the missing part or element of a given sequence or pattern. Common rules include:

Identify the Sequence: Look for a logical sequence in the pattern, whether it's numbers, letters, shapes, or symbols.

  • For example, in a numerical pattern, you may need to identify a consistent difference between consecutive numbers.
  • In a shape pattern, look for a consistent transformation (rotation, reflection, etc.) between successive shapes.

Consistency in Transformation: Patterns usually follow a consistent rule. Common transformations include:

  • Repetition: The same pattern repeats after a fixed number of elements (e.g., 1, 2, 1, 2, ...).
  • Rotation: The pattern may rotate by a certain degree (e.g., 90Â°, 180Â°) or move in a circular pattern.
  • Reflection: Shapes or symbols may be mirrored along an axis (vertical or horizontal).
